Naming conventions for detector elements


Translating between online- and offline-convention names might be confusing, especially when searching for an element by ID. Try using wildcards (*) or regular expressions (if you dare!) to locate an element.

Below are some basic guidelines to the conventions.

Offline convention

An example "offline ID" might look like:

     TGC_T4F1C24

  • TGC refers to the detector type. Possible types include TGC, RPC, CSC, and MDT. These are identified by the different colored rectangles on the plot.

  • T4F identifies the class of detector. It will usually be a 3-letter acronym like "BOS" or "CSL", which describes the location of the detector class.

  • 1C describes the eta (or z) position of the detector element: a smaller eta value will be closer to the interaction point. "A" refers to the +z side of the interaction point, and "C" refers to the -z side.

  • 24 indicates the phi sector. The labelling for these sectors will vary between detector types. In the offline convention, elements are typically indexed by a single number (1-indexed) that increases counterclockwise from the +z direction, and begins at the +x axis.

Online convention

The online convention follows a similar format. The main differences are:
  • Phi sector. This index is often counted in increments of 2, and may be odd or even depending on the detector type.
    • For most TGC's, the online convention specifies a separate "phi subsector" index, which is usually 0-indexed. This follows the element ID and an added underscore. E.g., the element TGC_I4E1C05_0 is in the first subsector (index 0) within phi-sector 5.
  • Eta sector.
    • For "BOF" and "BOG" classes of MDT's and RPC's, the eta index is again counted in increments of 2.
    • The eta convention is flipped for some TGC's: T1E, T2E, T3E (online: M1E, M2E, and M3E respectively) elements begin with eta = 1 farthest away from the beamline. Eta increases as the elements approach the beamline.
